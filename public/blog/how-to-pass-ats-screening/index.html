What ATS Screening Actually Does

An Applicant Tracking System is software that companies use to collect, organize, scan, and rank job applications. When you apply through a company's careers page, your resume does not go directly to a recruiter's inbox. Instead, it enters the ATS, where it is parsed, categorized, and scored against the job description.

Here is what happens step by step:

  1. Parsing: The ATS extracts text from your resume file and breaks it into structured fields: name, contact info, work history, education, skills. If your formatting is non-standard, the parser may misread or skip entire sections.
  2. Keyword matching: The system compares your resume content against the job posting's required qualifications, skills, and experience. It looks for exact and semantic keyword matches.
  3. Ranking and scoring: Based on keyword density, relevance, experience level, and other criteria set by the recruiter, the ATS assigns your application a compatibility score.
  4. Filtering: Recruiters typically review only the top-ranked candidates. If your score falls below the threshold, your resume may never be opened by a human.

Over 99% of Fortune 500 companies use some form of ATS, and adoption among mid-sized companies has surged in recent years. Understanding how to pass ATS screening is no longer optional for serious job seekers. It is a fundamental part of the modern job search.

The Major ATS Systems You Need to Know

Not all applicant tracking systems work identically. Each platform has its own parsing engine, scoring algorithm, and quirks. Knowing which ATS a company uses can help you fine-tune your approach. Here are the four most widely deployed systems in 2026:

Oracle Taleo

The legacy giant. Used by a significant share of Fortune 500 companies including large banks, healthcare systems, and government contractors. Taleo is strict with formatting and relies heavily on exact keyword matching. It struggles with tables, columns, and non-standard section headers.

Workday Recruiting

The enterprise favorite for companies that already run Workday HCM. Workday's parser has improved significantly but still performs best with clean, single-column resumes. It tends to weight recent experience and specific certifications heavily in its scoring.

Greenhouse

Ranked the top ATS in 2026 G2 reports. Popular with mid-market and fast-growing companies, particularly in tech. Greenhouse uses structured scorecards and offers better semantic matching than older systems. Still, formatting matters: fancy templates can trip up its parser.

Lever

Favored by startups and tech-forward organizations. Lever combines ATS and CRM functionality. Its parsing is relatively forgiving, but it still requires clean formatting. Lever places strong emphasis on skills matching and allows recruiters to set custom knockout questions.

Other systems you may encounter include iCIMS (common in healthcare and retail), SmartRecruiters, Jobvite, BambooHR, and Ashby (gaining traction among scaling startups). The optimization principles below apply across all of them.

10 Actionable Tips to Pass ATS Screening

These are the proven ATS screening tips that consistently help resumes make it through automated filters and into the hands of hiring managers.

1 Use a Clean, Single-Column Format

ATS parsers read resumes linearly, from top to bottom, left to right. Multi-column layouts, text boxes, tables, and sidebar designs may cause the system to scramble your content or skip sections entirely. Use a straightforward single-column layout with clearly defined sections. This is the single most important thing you can do to pass ATS resume screening.

2 Mirror Keywords From the Job Description

This is where most candidates lose points. The ATS compares your resume directly against the job posting. If the listing says "project management" and your resume says "managed projects," you may not get full credit. Read the job description carefully. Identify the hard skills, soft skills, certifications, and tools mentioned. Incorporate those exact phrases naturally into your experience bullets and skills section.

For example, if the job requires "cross-functional collaboration," use that exact phrase rather than paraphrasing it as "worked with different teams." Include both the spelled-out term and the acronym: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)," "Certified Public Accountant (CPA)."

3 Use Standard Section Headings

ATS systems rely on section headings to categorize your information correctly. Stick with conventional labels that every system recognizes:

  • Work Experience or Professional Experience
  • Education
  • Skills
  • Certifications
  • Summary or Professional Summary

Avoid creative alternatives like "Where I've Made an Impact," "My Toolbox," or "Career Journey." The ATS may not recognize them and could miscategorize or ignore that content entirely.

4 Submit the Right File Format

Most modern ATS platforms in 2026 can handle both .docx and .pdf files reliably. However, if the job posting does not specify a format, .docx is still the safest choice, especially for older systems like Taleo. Never submit image-based PDFs (scanned documents), .pages files, .rtf, or .jpg resumes. If you use a PDF, make sure the text is selectable, not embedded as an image.

5 Put Contact Information in the Body, Not the Header

Many resume templates place your name, email, and phone number in the document header or footer. The problem: several ATS platforms (Taleo in particular) cannot read header and footer content. Place your full name on the first line of the document body, followed by your phone number, email address, LinkedIn URL, and city/state. Keep it simple and parseable.

6 Tailor Your Resume for Every Application

A "one-size-fits-all" resume is one of the fastest ways to fail ATS screening. Each job has different requirements, and each ATS comparison is run against that specific posting. You do not need to rewrite your entire resume every time, but you should adjust your summary, reorder your skills to prioritize what the job emphasizes, and swap in relevant keywords from the description. This is the single most effective way to beat ATS systems consistently.

7 Quantify Your Achievements

While ATS primarily filters on keywords, recruiters who review the top-scoring resumes look for measurable impact. Replace vague descriptions with specific numbers:

  • Weak: "Responsible for improving sales performance"
  • Strong: "Increased regional sales by 23% ($1.2M) in Q3 2025 through targeted account-based marketing campaigns"

Quantified achievements also naturally incorporate action verbs and context that improve keyword relevance.

8 Use Standard Fonts and Simple Formatting

Stick with widely recognized fonts: Arial, Calibri, Georgia, Verdana, or Times New Roman in 10-12pt size. Avoid decorative fonts, custom icons, colored text, or complex formatting like condensed character spacing. Bold and bullet points are fine and encouraged. Underlining can interfere with how some parsers read lowercase letters with descenders (g, j, p, q, y).

9 Spell Out Abbreviations and Acronyms

Different ATS systems may search for either the full term or the abbreviation. Cover both by writing the full term followed by the acronym in parentheses the first time you use it: "Customer Relationship Management (CRM)," "International Business Machines (IBM)," "Certified Information Systems Security Professional (CISSP)." This doubles your chances of matching the recruiter's keyword filter.

10 Include a Skills Section With Hard Skills

Beyond weaving keywords into your experience bullets, include a dedicated Skills section that lists your technical competencies, tools, certifications, and relevant software. This gives the ATS a concentrated block of matchable keywords. But do not just dump a list of buzzwords. Every skill you list should be substantiated by your experience section. Modern ATS systems and AI-assisted recruiters can detect keyword stuffing, and it will hurt you.

Common Mistakes That Get Resumes Rejected

Even well-qualified candidates get filtered out by ATS because of avoidable errors. Here are the most damaging mistakes we see, based on analyzing thousands of resumes with our ATS checker tool:

Using tables, columns, or text boxes for layout.

This is the number one formatting killer. ATS parsers read content in a linear stream. When your resume uses a two-column layout or tables to arrange information, the system may merge unrelated content, skip columns entirely, or scramble the order. Your "Skills" sidebar might get concatenated with your job titles, creating nonsensical output. Always use a single-column format.

Embedding contact information in headers or footers.

If the ATS cannot read your header, it cannot extract your name, email, or phone number. You become an anonymous application. Multiple major systems, including older versions of Taleo and iCIMS, still have trouble parsing header and footer content reliably.

Submitting image-based or scanned PDFs.

If you scanned a printed resume or exported it as a flat image, the ATS sees a blank page with no extractable text. Always ensure your PDF contains selectable, searchable text. A quick test: try to highlight and copy text from your PDF. If you cannot, neither can the ATS.

Using graphics, icons, logos, or photos.

Profile photos, skill-level bar charts, company logos, and decorative icons are invisible to ATS. Worse, they can disrupt the parser's ability to read surrounding text. Save the visual design for a portfolio version of your resume that you hand to interviewers in person.

Keyword stuffing or hiding white text.

Some candidates try to beat the ATS system by pasting invisible white text loaded with keywords at the bottom of their resume. Modern ATS platforms detect this. Even if it gets past the software, any recruiter who selects all text will see your hidden block. This is an instant disqualification and a serious red flag.

Using the same resume for every application.

Every job posting has different priority keywords. A resume tailored for a "Marketing Manager" role will not score well against a "Digital Marketing Specialist" posting, even if the responsibilities overlap. Tailoring your resume to each application is the highest-impact change you can make.

Misspelling keywords and job titles.

ATS cannot interpret what you "meant" to type. If the job requires "Salesforce" and your resume says "Sales Force" or "SalesForce," you may lose that keyword match. Double-check the exact spelling of tools, technologies, certifications, and job titles against the job listing.

How to Test If Your Resume Passes ATS

You would not submit a final exam without reviewing your answers. Treat your resume the same way. Before you hit "Apply," run your resume through an ATS compatibility check to catch problems before they cost you the opportunity.

Here is what a good ATS test should evaluate:

  1. Parsing accuracy: Can the system correctly extract your name, contact info, job titles, dates, education, and skills? If the parser scrambles your content, a recruiter's ATS will too.
  2. Keyword match rate: How well does your resume align with the specific job description? Look for your match percentage and identify the exact keywords you are missing.
  3. Formatting compatibility: Are there tables, columns, headers, graphics, or other elements that could trip up the parser?
  4. Section recognition: Does the tool correctly identify your resume sections (experience, education, skills), or are they being miscategorized?

A Practical Workflow for Every Application

  1. Start with your master resume that includes all your experience and skills.
  2. Read the job description thoroughly. Highlight required skills, tools, certifications, and key phrases.
  3. Adjust your resume: rewrite your summary, reorder skills, and incorporate the highlighted keywords into your experience bullets.
  4. Run it through the ATScore scanner with the job description pasted in.
  5. Review your score and keyword gaps. Make targeted edits to close the gaps.
  6. Re-scan to confirm improvement, then submit.

This process adds about 10-15 minutes per application. But consider the alternative: sending hundreds of unoptimized resumes that get filtered out by ATS, wasting far more time and getting far fewer results.

The Bottom Line

Knowing how to pass ATS screening is not about gaming the system. It is about presenting your real qualifications in a format that both machines and humans can read and evaluate properly. The candidates who get interviews in 2026 are not necessarily more qualified. They are the ones whose resumes survive the automated filter.

Use clean formatting. Mirror the job description's language. Avoid the common mistakes that silently kill applications. And test your resume before every submission. These are not tricks to beat ATS systems. They are the baseline requirements for a professional job search in a world where software screens you before people do.
