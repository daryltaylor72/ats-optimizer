ATS Resume Guide for Entry-Level Job Seekers

Published April 13, 2026 · 7 min read · By ATScore

Most resume advice assumes you already have a career. When you're applying for your first real job -- fresh out of school, returning from a gap year, or making an early entry into the workforce -- the standard playbook doesn't fully apply. You can't fill five bullet points per role if you've never held one. But that doesn't mean your entry-level ATS resume has to come up short. It means you need to approach it differently.

Applicant tracking systems don't penalize candidates for having a short work history. They penalize resumes that are missing keywords, formatted incorrectly, or lack the structured data the parser expects. This guide walks you through every section of an ATS-friendly resume from scratch -- so you pass the initial screening and land in front of an actual human recruiter.

Why Entry-Level Resumes Fail ATS Screening

Entry-level candidates fail ATS screening for three consistent reasons: they omit sections the parser expects to find, they don't include enough keyword-matched content, or they use visually creative templates that look great on screen but parse into garbled output inside an ATS.

An ATS parses your resume into structured data fields -- contact info, work experience, education, skills, certifications. If a section is missing or formatted in a way the parser can't read, that field gets left blank or filled incorrectly. A slim resume with the right structure consistently outscores a dense resume with the wrong one. Understanding these failure points is how you build around them.

Section-by-Section: What to Include When You Have Little Experience

Contact Information

This section is identical for everyone, but entry-level candidates get it wrong in a specific way: they put their name and contact details in the document header. Most ATS platforms skip header and footer content during parsing entirely. Place your full name, professional email, phone number, city and state, and LinkedIn URL in the main body of the document. If you have a GitHub profile, portfolio site, or other relevant link, include it here as plain readable text -- no icons, no images, no embedded links.

Professional Summary

Entry-level candidates often skip the summary because they feel they have nothing to summarize. That's a missed opportunity. A two-to-three sentence summary gives the ATS a dense block of keyword context at the top of your document, and it signals to the recruiter exactly how you want to be positioned before they read a single bullet point.

Write the summary around your target role. If you're applying for marketing coordinator positions, lead with your relevant skills and education: "Recent marketing graduate with experience in social media content creation, email campaign management, and Google Analytics. Seeking an entry-level role where I can apply data-driven strategies to grow brand visibility." Two sentences, six job-relevant keywords -- that's how you open a strong entry-level ATS resume with no formal work history. See more examples in our ATS-friendly resume summary guide.

Education

For entry-level candidates, education carries more ATS weight than at any other career stage. List your degree name, institution, and graduation date clearly. If your GPA is above 3.5, include it. Below the basics, add a relevant coursework subsection -- three to six courses that align directly with the roles you're targeting. If you made the dean's list, completed an honors thesis, or received academic awards, include those too. ATS systems recognize these as legitimate data points, and recruiters actively look for them when filtering junior applicants.

Internships, Part-Time Work, and Campus Roles

Any internship you've completed should be formatted exactly like full-time work experience: title, organization, location, dates, and two to four bullet points that start with action verbs and include measurable results. "Assisted the social media team" is not a bullet point. "Wrote and scheduled 15 weekly Instagram posts that grew account engagement by 22% over a 12-week internship" is. The number doesn't have to be large -- it just has to be specific.

Part-time and retail jobs count too. A customer-facing role demonstrates communication, conflict resolution, and working under pressure. The key is framing: translate the role into the vocabulary of your target field. For the right language, browse our list of 150+ resume action verbs organized by skill category.

Projects

The Projects section is the most underused tool in any entry-level candidate's arsenal. Academic projects, personal builds, open-source contributions, and freelance work are all valid resume entries -- and ATS systems parse them exactly like work experience when formatted correctly.

For each project, include a descriptive title, the tools or technologies used, and one to two bullet points describing what you built and what the outcome was. If you built a web scraper in Python, say so. If you designed a marketing campaign for a class project, include the simulated reach metrics or the recognition it received. Give the ATS keywords to match against and give the recruiter a concrete example of your output.

Skills Section

Your skills section is the most direct way to feed keywords to an ATS, and it's where entry-level candidates can close keyword gaps that a thin work history creates. Present skills as a simple comma-separated list or a clean bulleted list -- never in a column layout, table, or with visual rating bars, which many parsers can't read correctly.

Match your skills to the exact language of the job description. If the posting says "Microsoft Excel," don't write "spreadsheets." If it says "project management," use that exact phrase. ATS keyword matching is often literal, and synonym handling is inconsistent across platforms like Workday, Greenhouse, and Taleo. For a full walkthrough, read our guide on how to write the skills section for ATS.

Entry-Level ATS Resume Checklist

  • Professional summary written around the target role with 4-5 relevant keywords
  • Education section includes GPA (if 3.5+), relevant coursework, and academic honors
  • All internships and part-time roles formatted with title, company, dates, and quantified bullets
  • Projects section includes specific tools used and measurable outcomes
  • Skills listed as plain text matching exact phrases from the job description
  • Single-column layout with no tables, text boxes, columns, or graphics
  • Standard section headings: Summary, Education, Experience, Projects, Skills
  • Contact information in the document body -- not in the header or footer
  • Saved as .docx or a text-selectable PDF

Which Keywords Matter Most for Entry-Level Roles

Entry-level roles share a common keyword vocabulary across industries. Recruiters screening junior candidates look for signals of trainability, communication ability, and foundational technical competence. These keyword categories appear consistently across ATS job matching for early-career positions:

  • Soft skills as actions: Don't list "teamwork" -- write "collaborated cross-functionally" or "co-led a four-person project team." The action framing is what gets matched.
  • Software and tools by exact name: Google Analytics, HubSpot, Salesforce, Python, SQL, Tableau, Adobe Creative Suite, Figma -- every relevant tool by its full product name.
  • Certifications and credentials: Google Digital Marketing Certificate, AWS Cloud Practitioner, CompTIA A+, Meta Blueprint -- list both the full name and the abbreviation on first mention.
  • Industry terminology: Use the specific vocabulary of the field. "A/B testing" not "experiment comparison." "Agile methodology" not "flexible process." "Financial modeling" not "spreadsheet analysis."

The Formatting Mistakes That Kill Entry-Level Resumes

Entry-level candidates are more likely than any other group to use visually creative templates -- Canva designs, multi-column Google Docs layouts, or infographic-style formats found online. These look polished as a PDF thumbnail but frequently parse into unreadable scrambled output inside an ATS.

The most common offenders are two-column layouts, tables used for structure, text boxes floating outside the document flow, and headers or footers containing contact information. If your resume was built in Canva or a graphically rich template, rebuild it in Microsoft Word or a plain Google Docs template before you apply. A single-column, reverse-chronological structure is the only format that parses reliably across all major ATS platforms.

Final Thoughts: No Experience Is Not a Disqualifier

Every recruiter who has ever filled an entry-level role has reviewed an entry-level resume. The benchmark is calibrated to where you are -- not to where a ten-year veteran is. Your job isn't to fake experience. It's to make the experience and skills you do have as visible and keyword-aligned as possible.

Use every applicable section: education, projects, internships, relevant coursework, skills, certifications. Write specific, results-oriented bullets for everything. Mirror the language of the job description. Keep your layout clean and machine-readable. And test your resume against each specific job before you submit -- not once at the start of your search, but every time you target a new role.
