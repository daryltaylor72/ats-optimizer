ATS-Ready Resume Checklist
23 Items That Matter

Every item on this list comes from analyzing thousands of real resume scans. These are the actual reasons applicant tracking systems filter out resumes before a human ever sees them.

01

Formatting & File Type

ATS parsers read your resume as structured text, not as a visual document. Layout choices that look good to humans often break machine parsing entirely.

  • Critical Use a single-column layout
    Multi-column layouts, text boxes, and tables cause ATS parsers to scramble your content. Sections end up interleaved or missing entirely. Use a clean, top-to-bottom single-column format.
  • Critical Submit as .docx or .pdf
    DOCX is the safest format for ATS parsing. PDF works for most modern systems but can cause issues with older ones. Never submit .doc, .pages, .jpg, or .png files.
  • Critical No images, graphics, or icons
    ATS cannot read text embedded in images. This includes headshots, skill-level bar charts, star ratings, custom icons, and infographic elements. All of that content is invisible to the parser.
  • Important Use standard fonts
    Stick to widely-supported fonts: Arial, Calibri, Cambria, Georgia, Helvetica, Times New Roman. Creative or decorative fonts can render as garbled characters in some parsers.
  • Important No headers or footers
    Content placed in Word/PDF header and footer regions is frequently skipped by ATS parsers. Your name, contact info, and page numbers should be in the main body, not in the header/footer.
  • Recommended Keep it under 2 pages
    Most ATS systems handle multi-page documents fine, but recruiters spend 6-8 seconds on initial review. Concise resumes score better on content density metrics.
  • Recommended Use 10-12pt font size
    Smaller fonts don't save space in ATS parsing (it ignores visual size), but they make human review harder. Stick to 10-12pt for body text, 14-16pt for section headings.
  • Recommended Save with a professional filename
    Use "FirstName-LastName-Resume.pdf" — not "resume_final_v3 (1).docx". Some ATS display the filename to recruiters. It's a small detail that signals professionalism.
02

Keywords & Job Matching

ATS systems score your resume by comparing your content against the job description. Missing the right keywords — even when you have the right experience — means automatic rejection.

  • Critical Mirror exact keywords from the job posting
    If the posting says "project management," don't write "managed projects." ATS keyword matching is often literal. Extract the specific terms, titles, and tools from the job description and use them verbatim.
  • Critical Include both acronyms and full terms
    Write "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)" the first time, then "SEO" afterward. Some systems search for the acronym, others for the full phrase. Cover both.
  • Critical Use a dedicated Skills section
    List your technical skills, tools, and certifications in a clearly labeled "Skills" or "Technical Skills" section. ATS parsers look for this section specifically when matching requirements.
  • Important Tailor for each application
    A generic resume that doesn't reflect the specific job's language will score low on keyword match. Adjust your skills section and bullet points for each role. Even 15 minutes of tailoring makes a measurable difference.
  • Important Use standard job titles
    Creative titles like "Growth Ninja" or "Chief Happiness Officer" don't match ATS keyword databases. Use the industry-standard equivalent, then clarify in the description if needed.
  • Recommended Don't keyword-stuff
    Repeating the same keyword 15 times doesn't help — modern ATS and recruiters both flag this. Use keywords naturally in context. 2-3 mentions of important terms across different sections is the sweet spot.
  • Recommended Include industry-specific certifications
    Certifications (PMP, AWS, CPA, Google Analytics, etc.) are high-weight keywords in many ATS systems. If you have them, list them prominently — they're often used as hard filters.
03

Structure & Content Quality

Even with the right format and keywords, poor structure or weak content will tank your score. ATS systems increasingly evaluate content quality, not just keyword presence.

  • Critical Use standard section headings
    Use exactly: "Experience" or "Work Experience," "Education," "Skills," "Summary" or "Professional Summary." Creative headings like "Where I've Made an Impact" confuse the parser — it won't map your content to the right fields.
  • Critical Include complete contact information
    Full name, email, phone number, city/state, and LinkedIn URL. Missing contact info means a recruiter can't reach you even if you score well. Put this at the top of the document body, not in a header.
  • Important Quantify your achievements
    "Increased sales by 34%" beats "Responsible for increasing sales." Numbers signal impact and are easy for both ATS and humans to parse. Add metrics to at least 50% of your bullet points.
  • Important Use reverse chronological order
    List your most recent role first. ATS systems expect this format and parse dates to calculate tenure. Functional or skills-based formats often confuse the parser's timeline extraction.
  • Important Include dates for every role
    Use a consistent format: "Jan 2022 – Present" or "2022 – 2024." Missing dates cause ATS to flag gaps or fail to calculate your years of experience, which many systems use as a hard filter.
  • Important Start bullet points with strong action verbs
    Lead with verbs like "Developed," "Managed," "Implemented," "Reduced," "Launched." Avoid starting with "Responsible for" or "Duties included" — these are passive and add no signal for ATS or recruiters.
  • Recommended Write a targeted professional summary
    A 2-3 sentence summary at the top gives the ATS another opportunity to match keywords and gives the recruiter immediate context. Tailor it to each role. Skip the "Objective" statement — it's outdated.
  • Recommended Remove "References available upon request"
    This wastes space and is assumed. Use that line for another keyword-rich bullet point or skill instead. Every line should earn its place on the page.
